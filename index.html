The Story

A five-step journey from audit to intelligence.

Step 1: Audit everything

We started by building MetaAudit — a battery of 11 independent detectors that systematically examined every meta-analysis in 501 Cochrane systematic reviews. Small-study effects, excess significance, heterogeneity outliers, p-hacking signals — 6,229 meta-analyses were put through a full quality audit. The results were sobering: quality issues are pervasive, not exceptional.

Step 2: Find the contradictions

Then we asked the uncomfortable question: do these meta-analyses even agree with each other? ContradictionMap identified 881 pairs of overlapping meta-analyses examining shared clinical questions. 48.9% showed discordant results — defined as directional reversal, significance discordance, or >2-fold magnitude difference. Most discordances (375 of 431) were significance flips, suggesting that differences in precision and power contribute substantially alongside genuine clinical disagreement.

Step 3: Filter for actionability

If evidence contradicts itself nearly half the time, what is actually ready to change clinical practice? ActionableEvidence scored all 6,229 meta-analyses against six pre-specified quality criteria. Only 1.3% met all thresholds simultaneously — though many that did not still contribute to clinical guidelines via GRADE assessment and other appraisal frameworks.

Step 4: Predict instability

EvidenceOracle used 48 features extracted from the MetaAudit dataset to build predictive models identifying meta-analyses at risk of instability. A logistic regression model achieved AUC 0.871 (random forest: 0.925) in 5-fold cross-validation on 403 reviews — promising discrimination, though external validation is needed before operational use.

Step 5: Score everything

Finally, EvidenceScore combines all signals — audit flags, contradiction exposure, actionability metrics, and stability predictions — into a single 0-100 composite trust score for each meta-analysis. One number that captures the full picture. A credit score for evidence.
